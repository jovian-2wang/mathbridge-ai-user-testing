Lesson 8 Summary
Sometimes we know the amount for
multiple
groups, but we don’t know how much is in one group. We can use division to find out.
For example: If 5 people share
8 1/2
pounds of cherries equally, how many pounds of cherries does each person get?
[Image description: A diagram showing how many pounds of cherries 5 people get]
We can represent this situation as a multiplication and a division:
5\, · {?} = 81/2
8 1/2 ÷ 5 = {?}
81/2 ÷ 5
can be written as
17/2 ÷ 5
. Dividing by 5 is equivalent to multiplying by
1/5
, and
17/2 · 1/5 =17/10
. This means each person gets
17/10
pounds.
Other times, we know the amount for
a fraction
of a group, but we don’t know the size of one whole group. We can also use division to find out.
For example: Jada poured 5 cups of iced tea in a pitcher and filled
2/3
of the pitcher. How many cups of iced tea fill the entire pitcher?
[Image description: A diagram showing how many cups fit in a pitcher]
We can represent this situation as a multiplication and a division:
2/3 · {?} = 5
5 ÷ 2/3 = {?}
The diagram can help us reason about the answer. If
2/3
of a pitcher is 5 cups, then
1/3
of a pitcher is half of 5, which is
5/2
. Because there are 3 thirds in 1 whole, there would be
(3 · 5/2)
or
15/2
cups in one whole pitcher. We can check our answer by multiplying:
2/3 · 15/2 = 30/6
, and
30/6 = 5
.
Notice that in the first example, the number of groups is greater than 1 (5 people) and in the second, the number of groups is less than 1 (
2/3
of a pitcher), but the division and multiplication equations for both have the same structures.
