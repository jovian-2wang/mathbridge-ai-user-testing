Lesson 3 Summary
If a situation involves equal-sized groups, it is helpful to make sense of it in terms of the number of groups, the size of each group, and the total amount. Here are three examples to help us better understand such situations.
Suppose we have 3 bottles with
61/2
ounces of water in each, and the total amount of water is not given. Here we have 3 groups,
61/2
ounces in each group, and an unknown total, as shown in this diagram:
[Image description: A tape diagram of 3 equal parts. Each part is labeled six and one half. A brace from the beginning of the diagram to the end of the disgram is labeled with a question mark.]
We can express this situation as a multiplication problem. The unknown is the product, so we can simply multiply the 2 known numbers to find it.
3 · 61/2 = {?}
Next, suppose we have 20 ounces of water to fill 6 equal-sized bottles, and the amount in each bottle is not given. Here we have 6 groups, an unknown amount in each, and a total of 20. We can represent it like this:
[Image description: A tape diagram of 6 equal parts. Each part is labeled with a question mark. A brace from the beginning of the diagram to the end of the diagram is labeled with 20.]
This situation can also be expressed using multiplication, but the unknown is a factor, rather than the product:
6\, · {?} = 20
To find the unknown, we cannot simply multiply, but we can think of it as a division problem:
20 ÷ 6 = \,?
Now, suppose we have 40 ounces of water to pour into bottles, 12 ounces in each bottle, but the number of bottles is not given. Here we have an unknown number of groups, 12 in each group, and a total of 40.
[Image description: A tape diagram with 3 parts. The first part and third part are equal and are labeled 12. The second part is labeled with a question mark. A brace from the beginning of the diagram to the end of the diagram is labeled 40.]
Again, we can think of this in terms of multiplication, with a different factor being the unknown:
? · 12 = 40
Likewise, we can use division to find the unknown:
40 ÷ 12 = \,?
Whenever we have a multiplication situation, one factor tells us
how many groups
there are, and the other factor tells us
how much is in each group
.
Sometimes we want to find the total. Sometimes we want to find how many groups there are. Sometimes we want to find how much is in each group. Anytime we want to find out how many groups there are or how much is in each group, we can represent the situation using division.
